Most agencies quote the same hourly rate whether the team has three open weeks or is buried for the next two months. That's backwards. The rate card should move with capacity, the same way airline ticket prices move with how full the plane is.

## The pattern I keep seeing

Talk to enough agency owners and a pattern shows up: the deals that get discounted hardest are the ones that come in when the team is slammed. Sales wants to close it, delivery is already stretched, and the easiest lever to pull is price. That's exactly the deal that should be priced at a premium — there's no slack left to absorb it cheaply.

The inverse happens too. When a team has open capacity, quotes still go out at the standard rate, and winnable deals get lost to a competitor pricing more aggressively to fill their own bench.

Both failures come from the same root cause: pricing decisions are made without looking at capacity data that already exists somewhere in a PM tool or time tracker.

## What capacity-based pricing actually looks like

It's simpler than it sounds. You need three things:

1. **A live capacity number** — utilization by role, by week, pulled from whatever tool already tracks time or project load.
2. **A pricing function** — a rule (or model) that maps capacity to a rate adjustment. Doesn't need to be complex to start: above 85% utilization, rates go up 10-15%; below 60%, rates flex down to win the next deal.
3. **A delivery mechanism** — the recommended rate range needs to show up where a quote actually gets built, not buried in a separate dashboard nobody checks before sending a proposal.

That third part is where most attempts at this die. The data exists, the logic isn't hard, but if it doesn't land in front of whoever's building the quote, it doesn't change behavior.

## Why this is an automation problem, not a strategy problem

Every agency owner I've talked to about this already intuitively knows their pricing should flex with capacity. The reason it doesn't happen isn't lack of strategy — it's that nobody has time to manually check utilization before every quote. This is exactly the kind of decision that should be computed automatically and pushed to where the work happens, instead of relying on someone remembering to check.

That's the difference between a smart idea and a system that actually changes outcomes: automation that removes the step where good intentions usually die.
