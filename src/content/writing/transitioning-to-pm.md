Most PM career advice is written for people at big tech companies in large markets. If you're in San Francisco and your current role is at Google, the path to a PM job is relatively well-mapped. If you're in Kathmandu and your current role is Data Analyst at a local holding company, the map looks very different.

This is the version I wish I'd had two years ago.

## Why it's actually harder and easier than you think

It's harder because:

- Your portfolio of work is less legible to international hiring managers. A project at ING Group means nothing to a recruiter in Amsterdam or Austin without context.
- You don't have the informal network that makes most PM hiring happen. Referrals are disproportionately how PM roles are filled.
- Time zone asymmetry makes remote work arrangements more fragile, especially for async-heavy teams that still require some overlap hours.
- Visa sponsorship is off the table for most companies hiring "remote" — they mean remote-in-their-country, not remote-from-anywhere.

It's easier because:

- The actual skills required for PM work — clear writing, structured thinking, cross-functional collaboration, product sense — are fully learnable from where you are. None of them require geography.
- Remote-first companies genuinely hire globally, and some of them are actively looking for candidates who can cover APAC timezones.
- The bar for portfolio work is lower than people think. A well-documented side project demonstrates more than a job title.
- Being the person who can operate across cultural contexts — useful if the company has users or operations in South Asia — is a specific edge, not a consolation prize.

## The foundation: building legible experience

The first challenge is building work that reads as PM work to someone who doesn't know your organisation.

This means documenting projects in the format that PMs use. If you led a data pipeline project, write a PRD-style document for it retroactively: problem statement, user segments, success metrics, decision log. If you ran a process improvement, write a case study that emphasises the discovery phase and the tradeoffs you made. If you built an internal tool, document it as a product.

This isn't dishonest. It's translation. The underlying work was PM work. The documentation makes it legible.

I started doing this six months into my role at ING, retrospectively documenting projects I'd been involved in. It took about two hours per project. Those documents are now the foundation of my portfolio.

## The skills gap map

Before you know what to learn, you need to know where you're weak. The PM skill set breaks down roughly as:

**Product sense** — understanding what makes a product good, and being able to evaluate product decisions with clear reasoning. This is developed by using products critically, reading case studies, and practising with frameworks like opportunity trees and jobs-to-be-done.

**User research** — knowing how to run discovery interviews, synthesise qualitative data, and translate user insights into product decisions. This is learnable with practice. I started by interviewing students at ING colleges about their academic experience. Free participants, real insights.

**Execution skills** — writing clear specs, running prioritisation exercises, managing stakeholders, working in agile sprints. These are mostly learnable by doing, ideally alongside a product team.

**Domain knowledge** — deep familiarity with the market, users, and technology in a specific vertical. This is where local context is an advantage. My understanding of how educational institutions in Nepal actually operate is not something a PM in London can replicate quickly.

**Communication** — clear writing, structured presentations, the ability to influence without authority. This is the one most people underestimate. If your written English is not at the level where you can write PRDs that engineering teams act on without follow-up questions, this is the first thing to fix.

## The portfolio approach

The strongest PM portfolio for someone in my position has three components:

**One documented past project** — something you led or heavily contributed to in your current role, written up as a product case study with problem, approach, decision rationale, and results.

**One side project** — something you built or designed from scratch. Doesn't need to be live or successful. Needs to show that you can go from problem to solution independently. This can be a product teardown with a redesign proposal, a tool you built for yourself, or a speculative product spec for a real problem you've observed.

**One analysis or writing sample** — something that demonstrates your thinking, research ability, and communication. A product teardown, a market analysis, or a series of posts like this one.

You don't need more than these three things to start applying. You need them to be high quality.

## Applying realistically

Certain types of roles are more accessible from this position than others:

**Remote-first startups** — especially Series A/B companies that have users in Asia and are looking for timezone coverage. Look for companies with distributed teams and async-first cultures.

**Product ops and associate PM roles** — a realistic first step that gets you into a product team and gives you legible experience faster than trying to jump straight to PM.

**Vertical-specific companies** — EdTech, FinTech, or HealthTech companies serving Southeast or South Asian markets. Your regional context is directly valuable.

**Platforms with a marketplace model** — companies that need someone who understands how to balance supply and demand across geographies.

The salary expectations conversation is real and worth having with yourself honestly. Remote PM roles that pay well exist. They're competitive. The path to them usually goes through lower-paying entry-level remote work first, or through building a strong enough portfolio that you can skip the queue.

## What I'm doing

I'm actively working on this transition. My timeline is to be in a remote PM role within 18 months of this writing. The steps I'm executing:

1. Ship internal product work at ING that's documented and legible as PM work
2. Build the writing habit — consistent public thinking
3. Complete a structured PM course and deepen Agile practice
4. Apply to 10 associate PM roles at remote-first companies in Q3 2026
5. Iterate on what doesn't work

The honest truth is that I don't know if this timeline is right. But I know the direction is right, and that's enough to keep moving.
